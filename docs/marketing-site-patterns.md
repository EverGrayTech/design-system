# Marketing Site Composition and CTA Patterns Guidance

This document defines shared design-system guidance for EverGray Tech marketing-site composition, recurring public-facing section structures, and CTA hierarchy. It enables `evergraytech.com` and future brand surfaces to build consistent pages without inventing a parallel local visual grammar.

This guidance follows the shared posture defined in [System Spec](system-spec.md): the website is the slightly more cinematic expression of the system, but it remains typography-led, structure-first, and restrained. Marketing pages may feel more spacious and atmospheric than applications, yet they must still read as EverGray Tech — precise, technical, calm, and premium through control rather than spectacle.

---

## 1. Composition Model for Marketing Pages

Marketing pages should be assembled from recurring section types with clear roles. The system does not mandate one landing-page template, but it does define the kinds of sections that may recur and how they should behave.

### 1.1 Core Section Types

An EverGray Tech marketing page will usually draw from the following section types:

1. **Hero section** — the page's highest-emphasis entry point.
2. **Intro or value section** — clarifies what EverGray Tech does and why it matters.
3. **Capability or service group** — organizes offerings, competencies, or product pillars.
4. **Proof section** — credibility, leadership, evidence, technical depth, or operational trust.
5. **CTA section** — focused conversion or next-step framing.
6. **Footer** — final navigation, contact, and company framing.

Pages may omit, reorder, or repeat section types when necessary, but each section should have one dominant job. Avoid sections that try to explain the company, prove credibility, list services, and ask for action simultaneously.

### 1.2 Section Posture by Type

#### Hero Section

**Role:** Establish the strongest value proposition, brand posture, and first-action path.

**Expected contents:**
- eyebrow or kicker (optional)
- primary headline
- supporting lead paragraph
- primary CTA and optional secondary CTA
- optional restrained proof cue or supporting metadata

**Posture:**
- This is the most spacious and visually energized area on the page.
- It may use atmospheric background treatments defined in [Website Brand Atmosphere, Graphics, and Motion Restraint Guidance](website-brand-atmosphere-and-graphics.md).
- The hero should still feel content-led. Atmosphere supports the headline; it never replaces it.

#### Intro or Value Section

**Role:** Translate the hero's broad promise into a more concrete explanation.

**Expected contents:**
- short section kicker or label
- section title
- concise supporting paragraph
- optional grouped value points or short bullets

**Posture:**
- Usually quieter than the hero.
- Often uses spacing and typography rather than elevated panels.
- Should feel like a clean continuation, not a second hero.

#### Capability or Service Group

**Role:** Present offerings, system capabilities, or structured areas of expertise.

**Expected contents:**
- section heading
- brief orienting description
- grouped list, grid, or stacked capability cards
- optional supporting metadata or links per item

**Posture:**
- Structured, scan-friendly, and grid-led.
- Emphasis should come from ordering, spacing, and titles.
- Cards or panels may be used, but they should remain restrained and embedded in the overall surface model.

#### Proof Section

**Role:** Build confidence through evidence, operational maturity, leadership credibility, or implementation substance.

**Acceptable proof forms:**
- customer or partner proof
- technical capability statements
- leadership credibility
- process discipline
- metrics, outcomes, or adoption indicators
- product or systems evidence

**Posture:**
- Proof should feel sober and specific.
- Prefer concise, structured evidence blocks over oversized testimonial theater.
- Use restraint: one strong proof structure is better than many weak badges or quotes.

#### CTA Section

**Role:** Convert attention into one clear next step.

**Expected contents:**
- short framing headline
- optional supporting sentence
- one primary action
- optional secondary or contact-oriented action

**Posture:**
- Clear and decisive.
- May use a slightly stronger section boundary, subtle atmospheric emphasis, or contained surface treatment.
- Must not resemble a promotional banner or bright sign-off card.

#### Footer

**Role:** Close the page with orientation, utility navigation, and company contact context.

**Expected contents:**
- logo or company name
- grouped navigation links
- contact or inquiry route
- legal or secondary metadata

**Posture:**
- Quiet, structural, and final.
- Footer composition should de-escalate from the CTA rather than competing with it.

### 1.3 Composition Principles Across Sections

- **One section, one job.** Each section should have a dominant communicative purpose.
- **Escalate, then resolve.** Early sections establish value, middle sections add substance, later sections narrow toward action.
- **Use containment selectively.** Not every section needs cards, panels, or framed blocks. Flat canvas sections are often the right baseline.
- **Let structure do the work.** A premium technical page feels composed because the sections relate cleanly, not because every section is visually unique.
- **Avoid landing-page sameness.** Do not repeat identical centered headline + three-card + button patterns section after section.

## 2. Section Hierarchy and Content Framing

Marketing surfaces should create emphasis through typography, spacing, contrast, and layout sequence. Decorative styling is secondary.

### 2.1 Content Hierarchy Within a Section

The preferred hierarchy for most marketing sections is:

1. **Eyebrow / kicker** — brief category or orientation cue.
2. **Primary section title** — the dominant text for the section.
3. **Lead paragraph** — a concise expansion of the title.
4. **Body or supporting details** — explanatory text, grouped bullets, or item descriptions.
5. **Metadata / support cues** — labels, small stats, contextual notes, or linked references.

This order may compress or simplify, but sections should not invert it casually. For example, metadata should never visually outrank the section title.

### 2.2 Eyebrows and Kickers

Eyebrow text is optional and should be used when it improves orientation.

**Appropriate uses:**
- categorizing a section (`Platform`, `Capabilities`, `Trusted by teams`)
- distinguishing proof from explanation
- clarifying a section's role on longer pages

**Rules:**
- Keep it short — usually one line.
- Use restrained contrast and spacing.
- Do not style it as a bright badge or pill by default.
- It should support the headline, not compete with it.

### 2.3 Headlines and Lead Copy

Headlines carry authority on marketing pages. They may be larger than application surfaces, but they should remain precise and composed.

**Rules:**
- Headlines should wrap cleanly into deliberate line lengths.
- Prefer 2–4 lines on smaller widths and 1–3 lines on wider layouts when possible.
- Lead paragraphs should stay visibly subordinate to the headline.
- Body copy should not attempt to carry brand drama through flourish or excess length.

### 2.4 Grouped Lists and Structured Content

Capability lists, feature groups, and service collections should feel engineered rather than promotional.

**Recommended patterns:**
- titled list rows
- restrained cards with short descriptions
- two-column or three-column grouped structures
- stacked capability blocks with supporting metadata

**Rules:**
- Use headings to create scan landmarks.
- Keep description lengths reasonably consistent within a group.
- If one item requires much more explanation than others, it may need its own section.
- Avoid over-decoration such as oversized icons, gradient fills, or deep card stacks.

### 2.5 Supporting Metadata

Marketing sections may use metadata to increase specificity and technical credibility.

**Examples:**
- sector or service labels
- engagement model descriptors
- implementation notes
- compact metrics or maturity indicators
- location, timeline, or availability cues

**Rules:**
- Metadata should read as supporting context, not a visual headline.
- Use smaller type, quieter color, and tighter grouping.
- If metadata becomes the main reason the section is readable, the section hierarchy is inverted and should be reworked.

### 2.6 Section Boundaries and Transitions

Section separation should come from one or more of the following:

1. **Spacing alone** — the default choice when sections are already distinct.
2. **Tonal step** — switching between canvas and a slightly raised or toned section background.
3. **Structural divider** — a thin line or geometric separator when density requires it.
4. **Containment change** — moving from open layout to contained panels or vice versa.

**Rules:**
- Prefer spacing first.
- Use borders and dividers when they clarify structure, not as constant decoration.
- Avoid stacking every section boundary with spacing + border + background change simultaneously unless the page is unusually dense.

## 3. CTA and Link-Emphasis Hierarchy

Calls to action on marketing pages should be unmistakable without turning the page into a sequence of sales banners. EverGray's CTA system distinguishes between action levels clearly.

### 3.1 CTA Tiers

#### Primary CTA

**Role:** The main next step on a page or within a high-priority section.

**Examples:**
- `Get Started`
- `Book a Consultation`
- `Request a Demo`
- `Contact EverGray`

**Rules:**
- Use one primary CTA per section cluster.
- The hero and the concluding CTA section may each have a primary action.
- Primary CTAs may use brand-accent treatment because they are true emphasis moments.
- If multiple actions appear side by side, only one should read as primary.

#### Secondary CTA

**Role:** An alternate action for users who are interested but not ready for the primary conversion path.

**Examples:**
- `View Services`
- `See Platform Overview`
- `Read the Docs`

**Rules:**
- Secondary CTAs should be visually subordinate to the primary action.
- They may use neutral outline, ghost, or quiet filled treatments.
- They should remain clearly interactive, but they must not visually tie with the primary CTA.

#### Quiet / Tertiary Action

**Role:** Low-emphasis supportive navigation or optional next steps.

**Examples:**
- `Learn more`
- `See all capabilities`
- `View case studies`

**Rules:**
- Quiet actions are usually text-forward and neutral.
- They should often appear below or adjacent to content, not dominate the section chrome.
- Use them to preserve flexibility without creating decision clutter.

#### Inline Text Link

**Role:** Connect explanatory copy to a related detail or destination.

**Rules:**
- Inline links should remain readable within paragraphs.
- They should not mimic primary CTA styling.
- Use them for reference movement, not as the central conversion mechanism of a page.

#### Contact-Oriented Action

**Role:** Provide direct outreach paths that may be more consultative than transactional.

**Examples:**
- `Email us`
- `Talk to our team`
- `Schedule a call`

**Rules:**
- Contact actions may appear as primary or secondary depending on the page goal.
- When a page is consultative rather than product-led, contact may be the primary CTA.
- Contact-oriented actions should still feel designed, not like an afterthought mailto link dropped into the footer.

### 3.2 When to Use Brand Accent vs. Neutral Treatment

The brand accent is reserved for real emphasis.

**Use accent treatment for:**
- the hero's primary CTA
- the page's main concluding CTA
- a singular high-priority action within a conversion-focused section

**Use neutral treatment for:**
- secondary actions
- supporting navigation within informational sections
- repeated card-level links across a capability grid
- low-priority or exploratory actions

**Rule:** if everything uses accent, nothing is prioritized. Accent is not the default action styling for all buttons on a marketing page.

### 3.3 CTA Grouping Rules

- Keep CTA groups compact and deliberate.
- Two actions is a normal maximum in a hero or CTA cluster.
- Three actions may be acceptable only when the third is clearly tertiary.
- If a section needs more than three actions, the content model is probably unresolved.
- Repeated lists or cards should usually expose one link each, not full multi-button action clusters.

### 3.4 Interaction-State Expectations

Marketing interaction states should feel crisp and controlled.

#### Hover
- Primary actions may slightly shift background, border, or shadow intensity.
- Secondary and quiet actions may increase text and border contrast.
- Text links may brighten and optionally add an understated underline or offset underline.
- Do not use hover scaling on text links or dramatic button lifts.

#### Focus
- Focus must be obvious and accessible.
- Use a visible focus ring or outline that remains consistent with the system's cool, precise character.
- Focus treatment should not depend solely on color shift.

#### Active / Pressed
- Use a slight tonal compression or inset feeling.
- Keep active states subtle and responsive.
- Do not create rubbery motion, bounce, or exaggerated press depth.

#### Motion
- CTA transitions should respect the motion guidance in [Website Brand Atmosphere, Graphics, and Motion Restraint Guidance](website-brand-atmosphere-and-graphics.md).
- Motion supports interaction confidence; it should never create spectacle.

## 4. Responsive Posture for Marketing Patterns

Marketing pages should adapt by preserving hierarchy and composure, not by simply shrinking desktop layouts.

### 4.1 Headline Wrapping and Width Control

- Headlines should wrap earlier on narrow screens to maintain readable line lengths.
- Keep headline blocks visually intentional; avoid single-word orphan lines when possible.
- Supporting lead copy should remain comfortably narrower than the full viewport.
- On very wide screens, do not let hero copy stretch indefinitely; keep the text block constrained so the composition remains focused.

### 4.2 Section Spacing Changes

- Reduce vertical spacing on smaller screens, but preserve clear separation between sections.
- Compress supporting whitespace before reducing text clarity.
- CTA clusters and proof groupings should stay visually grouped even after spacing tightens.
- Mobile pages should still feel calm and premium, not compressed into dense stacked banners.

### 4.3 Multi-Column to Stacked Behavior

- Capability grids, proof blocks, and service groupings may collapse from multi-column to stacked layouts.
- When stacking, preserve the same reading order implied on desktop.
- Stacked items should gain enough spacing to avoid reading as one long undifferentiated list.
- If a side-by-side proof or leadership layout becomes too long when stacked, simplify or trim nonessential secondary content.

### 4.4 CTA Regrouping on Smaller Screens

- Side-by-side CTA buttons may stack vertically when width is limited.
- Preserve CTA priority when stacking: primary first, then secondary, then tertiary.
- Avoid wrapping two buttons awkwardly across two rows with mismatched widths.
- Quiet actions may move below a main CTA group as text links when that improves clarity.

### 4.5 Contact and Information Cards

- Contact cards, inquiry blocks, or support metadata may simplify to single-column layouts on narrow screens.
- Preserve clear labels and scannable structure.
- Reduce ornamental framing before reducing padding below readable thresholds.
- If multiple contact methods appear together, keep the recommended path visually first.

### 4.6 Simplification Rules for Narrow Viewports

The following structures may simplify on narrow screens:

- supporting metadata may reduce in count or move beneath primary content
- decorative or optional dividers may be removed
- side annotations may collapse into the main text flow
- multi-column proof compositions may stack or convert into a sequence of simpler blocks

The following should **not** be sacrificed:

- headline clarity
- CTA priority
- section boundaries
- readable spacing around grouped information
- contact or next-step clarity

## 5. Website-Specific Dos and Don'ts

Marketing pages can feel more atmospheric than applications, but they must stay inside EverGray's boundaries.

### 5.1 Do

- Use spacious composition to create authority and calm.
- Let typography, alignment, and section rhythm carry hierarchy.
- Use atmospheric treatments only where they reinforce focal structure.
- Use cards and panels selectively for grouped content, proof, or CTA emphasis.
- Let borders and dividers feel machined and structural.
- Reuse recurring section logic across pages so the website feels systematized rather than improvised.

### 5.2 Don't

- Don't turn every section into a card or contained panel.
- Don't use glassy surfaces, heavy blur, or glow-led emphasis.
- Don't rely on bright gradients or decorative illustration to make sections feel premium.
- Don't create generic SaaS landing-page patterns with oversized badges, inflated pills, and constant card stacks.
- Don't add complex marketing chrome just to make the page feel “designed.”
- Don't let atmospheric brand treatment weaken text contrast, hierarchy, or scanability.

### 5.3 Boundaries for Card Treatment and Dividers

**Cards are appropriate when:**
- grouping capabilities or offerings
- isolating proof or testimonial-style evidence
- framing a CTA cluster that needs local emphasis
- presenting contact or inquiry paths clearly

**Cards are not appropriate when:**
- used as the default wrapper for every paragraph block
- stacked repeatedly to manufacture depth
- combined with heavy shadow, bright border, and tinted fills simultaneously

**Dividers are appropriate when:**
- a dense page needs section landmarks
- a footer needs grouped navigation clarity
- proof or data-oriented rows need structure

**Dividers are not appropriate when:**
- they appear between every small block of text
- they are decorative lines with no structural job
- they compete with spacing that already resolves the grouping

### 5.4 Relationship to Website Atmosphere Guidance

Where background treatments, subtle graphics, or motion overlap with composition, this document should be used alongside [Website Brand Atmosphere, Graphics, and Motion Restraint Guidance](website-brand-atmosphere-and-graphics.md).

**Practical rule:** composition decides the structure; atmosphere may reinforce it but never define it.

## 6. Reference Structures and Examples

These examples describe the intended structure of common marketing patterns. They are reference compositions, not rigid templates.

### 6.1 Hero Structure

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Nav (quiet, structural)                                            │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                    │
│  Kicker                                                            │
│  Primary Headline That Establishes the Core Value Proposition      │
│  Supporting lead paragraph that clarifies the promise and posture. │
│                                                                    │
│  [ Primary CTA ]   [ Secondary CTA ]                               │
│                                                                    │
│  Optional proof cue / metadata / trusted-by signal                 │
│                                                                    │
│                                    restrained atmosphere / graphic │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Notes:**
- The copy block should remain the dominant focal point.
- Any supporting graphic must stay secondary to the text and CTA cluster.
- Proof cues should not turn the hero into a logo wall.

### 6.2 Intro or Value Section

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Section kicker                                                     │
│ Section title                                                      │
│ Lead paragraph                                                     │
│                                                                    │
│  Value point A      Value point B      Value point C               │
│  short support      short support      short support               │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Notes:**
- This section often works without cards.
- Spacing and typographic grouping should carry the emphasis.

### 6.3 Proof Block

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Proof kicker                                                       │
│ Why teams trust EverGray Tech                                           │
│                                                                    │
│  Proof item / quote / metric      Supporting explanation           │
│  Proof item / quote / metric      Supporting explanation           │
│  Proof item / quote / metric      Supporting explanation           │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Notes:**
- Keep proof blocks structured and scannable.
- Prefer specificity over promotional adjectives.

### 6.4 Capability Group

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Capabilities                                                       │
│ What EverGray Tech can design, build, or operationalize                 │
│                                                                    │
│  ┌────────────────┐  ┌────────────────┐  ┌────────────────┐        │
│  │ Capability A   │  │ Capability B   │  │ Capability C   │        │
│  │ short desc     │  │ short desc     │  │ short desc     │        │
│  │ quiet link     │  │ quiet link     │  │ quiet link     │        │
│  └────────────────┘  └────────────────┘  └────────────────┘        │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Notes:**
- Repeated items should maintain a consistent internal hierarchy.
- Quiet links usually outperform repeated high-emphasis buttons in a grid.

### 6.5 CTA Section

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Ready to move faster with more precision?                          │
│ Brief supporting sentence.                                         │
│                                                                    │
│ [ Primary CTA ]   [ Contact / Secondary CTA ]                      │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Notes:**
- The section should feel resolved and intentional.
- Avoid adding unrelated navigation, badges, or proof clutter around the final CTA.

### 6.6 Mobile Adaptation Reference

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Kicker                               │
│ Primary headline wraps into          │
│ deliberate short lines               │
│ Supporting lead paragraph            │
│                                      │
│ [ Primary CTA ]                      │
│ [ Secondary CTA ]                    │
│                                      │
│ proof cue or metadata                │
└──────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Mobile rules shown here:**
- CTA group stacks in priority order.
- Headline remains visually dominant.
- Optional proof compresses beneath action rather than competing beside it.

### 6.7 Optional Naming for Reuse

Downstream sites may name recurring patterns if that improves consistency. Recommended names should stay descriptive rather than component-library-heavy.

Examples:
- `Marketing Hero`
- `Value Introduction`
- `Capability Grid`
- `Proof Strip`
- `CTA Closure`

These names are organizational aids only. They do not imply a required shared frontend implementation.
